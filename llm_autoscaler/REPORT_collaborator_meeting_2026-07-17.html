Bottom line

LLM-driven controllers beat both rule-based baselines (HPA, KEDA) on essentially every axis we measured — tail latency, mean latency, cost, and scaling churn — without any workload-specific training. They do not beat the trained RL baselines (DQN, PPO) on raw latency, which stay 4–6× faster at the median because they carry no LLM inference call in the control loop.

01Methodology

Every number in this report comes from a real k3s cluster (5 worker nodes), not the paper's hardened simulator. Each configuration drives a CPU-bound Flask app through a load trace for 120 steps × 60s, while the controller under test polls cluster state and issues real kubectl scale commands once per step. Two traces: cpu_bursty (synthetic, sharp bursts) and wiki_diurnal (real Wikipedia hourly traffic, diurnal + weekly pattern). Every configuration is repeated N=3 times. Replica ceiling is capped at 14 (shared-cluster ceiling, sized to clear both traces' ~2,199 rps peak).

Controllers under test

  • Baselines — HPA and KEDA, hand-implemented rule-based decisions (real k8s scaling, not simulated controllers)
  • RL — DQN and PPO, trained offline on the hardened simulator (500K steps), deployed with frozen weights, zero online adaptation
  • LLM — Llama-8B, Llama-70B, Mistral-Small, Qwen3-80B (being retired, see §7), GPT-OSS-120B (new, replacing Qwen), each in 4 prompt variants: zero_shot / domain / history_5 / cot

Cost model

Every controller charged identically: 0.25 vCPU per ready replica, integrated over actual wall-clock time between steps (not a fixed step duration — this absorbs real desync when a slow LLM call stretches a step past 60s). Annualized at $0.04/vCPU-hour (AWS on-demand, mid-range), per single service.

04Cross-cutting insights

HPA is cheap only because it under-provisions

HPA has the lowest cpu_bursty cost ($181/yr) and lowest average replica count (2.06) of anything in the study — but that's exactly why its P99 is 495ms and its mean/median gap is the widest of any controller (mean 106ms vs median 61ms). It looks fine on a quick glance at the median and falls apart on the tail.

KEDA is the worst all-around performer

Highest churn (53.0 scale-events/run), highest replica footprint (12.2 avg), highest cost ($1,069/yr) on cpu_bursty — and still loses on P99 to every mid-tier LLM. Every LLM controller we tested beats KEDA on cost, latency, and churn simultaneously.

Mistral-Small / Llama-70B are the sweet spot

Best cost/latency/churn balance among LLMs on cpu_bursty: mid-pack replicas, low churn (6.8 / 11.1 events per run), 100% success, competitive P99. This is the pairing to lead with if asked "which LLM config would you actually deploy."

LLM decision time varies by 100×, but isn't in the decision space right now

Qwen3-80B averages 135s per decision, Llama-70B 52s — both far past the 60s control interval. Mistral-Small and Llama-8B stay under 2s; GPT-OSS-120B is inconsistent (2.1s on zero_shot vs 16–26s on domain/history_5, averaging 14.9s). This is real and tracked, but by agreement it's not being weighted as a separate factor in the paper right now: the measured end-to-end request latency (P50/P90/P99) already reflects a slow decision empirically, so it doesn't need double-counting. Worth a callout if the collaborator asks about reactivity.

New today: GPT-OSS-120B/cot oscillates and misses SLA badly

Reproducible across all 3 cpu_bursty reps: the cot variant repeatedly decides "scale to 14," then within 1–2 steps decides "scale to 1" while the pod is still catching up — driving P99 to ~3,800ms and success rate down to 63% (vs P99 121–338ms / ~99% success for its other 3 variants). Every other model's cot variant is stable, so this looks specific to GPT-OSS-120B's chain-of-thought reasoning under this prompt, not a cluster or harness bug. Excluded from GPT-OSS-120B's headline numbers above; needs a root-cause look before it goes in the paper either as a data point or a caveat.

05The RL-DQN churn bug — found yesterday, fixed & re-run complete

Worth walking the collaborator through this — it's why the LLM-vs-RL stability comparison should be treated as still-forming rather than closed in this report.

  1. Symptom: RL-DQN's real-cluster scaling churn looked implausibly high — 92.7 scale-events/run on wiki_diurnal, 37.3/run on cpu_bursty — worse than every LLM config, which didn't square with RL's excellent latency numbers.
  2. Root cause: k8s_autoscaler.py's rl_decision() normalized its "how full am I" observation against a hardcoded max_replicas = 20 — the simulator's training-time cap — instead of the real runtime cap of 14 used by the real-cluster sweep. At the real cap, the policy's fullness signal read 14/20 = 0.70, never saturated, so it kept requesting replica counts above 14.
  3. Mechanism: scale_deployment() silently clamped over-cap requests to 14 before touching the cluster — but the unclamped decision was still what got compared against the next polled state, and what got written to the CSV. Every step the policy stayed "stuck" above the cap, the mismatch fired a redundant, no-op kubectl scale — inflating churn without any real cluster change.
  4. RL-PPO checked separately — clean. Its deterministic policy converges to a constant action of 8 on both traces, always under the 14 cap, so it never triggers the mismatch loop. Its numbers in this report are unaffected.
  5. Fix: rl_decision() now reads the real REPLICA_MAX at runtime and clamps its own return value — matching what hpa_decision(), keda_decision(), and the LLM decision path already did.
  6. Re-run complete. All 6 RL-DQN configs (cpu_bursty + wiki_diurnal × 3 reps) archived — not deleted — and re-run on a freshly isolated deployment (workload-cpu-7, idle node). All 6/6 now done, full N=3 on both traces, as of this snapshot.
